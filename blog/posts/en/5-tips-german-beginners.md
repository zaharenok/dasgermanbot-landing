# 5 Tips for German Beginners That Actually Work

Starting German can feel overwhelming. Long words, cases, genders — where do you even begin? Here are five tips that actually make a difference.

## 1. Learn the Most Common 500 Words First

You don't need thousands of words to start communicating. The 500 most frequent German words cover about 80% of everyday conversation. Focus on these first — words like *ich*, *du*, *sein*, *haben*, *machen* — and build from there.

## 2. Don't Fear the Long Words

*Lebensversicherungsgesellschaft* looks scary, but German compound words are just smaller words glued together. Break them apart: *Leben* (life) + *Versicherung* (insurance) + *Gesellschaft* (company). Once you see the pattern, long words become fun.

## 3. Nail the Pronunciation Early

German pronunciation is actually more consistent than English. Learn the sounds once and you'll rarely be surprised. Key ones to master:

- **ü** — say "ee" while rounding your lips
- **ö** — say "eh" while rounding your lips
- **ch** — like a soft hiss, not a hard "k"
- **ß** — just a sharp "ss"

## 4. Use It or Lose It

Passive studying (reading grammar books, scrolling flashcards) only gets you so far. You need to *produce* the language. Chat with a bot, write a diary entry, talk to yourself in German. Active recall is what builds fluency.

## 5. Five Minutes > Zero Minutes

A common trap: "I don't have 30 minutes today, so I'll skip." Bad move. Five minutes of focused practice every day beats a two-hour session once a week. Consistency compounds.
