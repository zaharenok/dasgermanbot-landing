# German False Friends: Words That Look English But Aren't

You confidently tell your German host "I'm pregnant" when you meant "I'm full." Or you announce at a dinner party that you "became impotent" instead of "became important."

False friends — words that look similar in two languages but mean different things — are the language learner's worst nightmare. And German has some absolute doozies that can turn a polite conversation into an awkward moment in seconds.

Let's fix that. Here are the German-English false friends you actually need to know.

## The Absolute Worst Offenders

**Gift vs Gift**
- German *Gift* = poison
- English *gift* = present
- *Pro tip:* Don't bring "Gift" to a birthday party unless you hate the person.

**bekommen vs become**
- German *bekommen* = to receive/get
- English *become* = to turn into
- *Classic mistake:* "Ich bekomme ein Kind" = "I'm *receiving* a child" (not "becoming" one, though it works out)

**Hell vs Hell**
- German *hell* = bright/light
- English *hell* = the underworld
- *Context:* "Das ist hell" = "That's bright" (not a comment on damnation)

## The Food-Related Disasters

**Chef vs Chef**
- German *Chef* = boss
- English *chef* = cook
- *Pro tip:* Your restaurant's *Chef* probably can't cook.

**Messer vs Mess**
- German *Messer* = knife
- English *mess* = disorder
- *Not a verb:* "Das Messer" = "the knife" (not "the mess")

**Stuhl vs Stool**
- German *Stuhl* = chair
- English *stool* = small seat
- *Both true:* A *Stuhl* is a chair, but *Hocker* is a stool.

## The "Wait, What?" Category

**Krampf vs Cramp**
- German *Krampf* = cramp/spasm
- English *cramp* = muscle contraction
- *Same meaning:* Actually, this one's fine. Moving on.

**Domestic vs Domestiziert**
- English *domestic* = household/internal
- German *domestiziert* = tamed (animals)
- *Don't use:* German doesn't really use "domestic" in the same way.

**fatal vs fatal**
- German *fatal* = unfortunate/serious (but not deadly)
- English *fatal* = deadly
- *Difference:* German *fatal* is bad, but *tödlich* is deadly.

## How to Avoid False Friend Traps

**1. Context is everything**
The sentence structure usually gives it away. If someone offers you *Gift* at a party, check if they're holding a box or laughing ominously.

**2. Learn the pairs together**
Don't just learn words — learn the false friend pair. *Gift/Poison*, *bekommen/receive*. Your brain will catch the pattern.

**3. Use a dictionary that shows false friends**
Some apps specifically flag false friends. Use them.

**4. When in doubt, ask**
Germans find false friend mistakes hilarious, not offensive. They've heard them all.

## The Ultimate Test

Read this sentence and spot the false friends:

*"After the dinner, the Chef gave me a Gift because I became pregnant."*

What you meant: *"After dinner, the cook gave me a present because I got full."*

What you said: *"After dinner, the boss gave me poison because I got pregnant."*

See the problem?

## Bottom Line

False friends are annoying, but they're also some of the most entertaining mistakes you'll make as a German learner. Embrace them. Laugh at them. And maybe keep a list handy until you've memorized the dangerous ones.

The good news? Once you know them, you'll never make those mistakes again. The bad news? German has hundreds more where these came from.

But start with these. They're the ones that'll actually get you in trouble.
