# German Word Order: Simple Explanation

German word order scares beginners. But it actually revolves around two simple rules. Remember them — and you'll understand 90% of sentences.

## Rule 1: Verb is always second

In a German main clause, the verb is **always** second. Not third, not fourth — exactly second.

What can be first? Anything:

- **Ich** *lerne* Deutsch. — I learn German.
- **Heute** *lerne* ich Deutsch. — Today I learn German.
- **Deutsch** *lerne* ich heute. — German I learn today.

See the pattern? The verb *lerne* is always second, no matter what comes first.

## Rule 2: Second verb part goes to the end

If the verb is complex (perfect, modal, future), its conjugated part stands second, and the second part goes to the end.

- **Ich** *habe* gestern Berlin *besucht*. — I visited Berlin yesterday.
- **Ich** *muss* heute Deutsch *lernen*. — I must learn German today.
- **Ich** *werde* morgen Deutsch *lernen*. — I will learn German tomorrow.

The sentence builds like a "frame": first verb part at the start, second part at the end. Between them — everything else.

## Subordinate clauses: verb at the end

After words *dass, weil, wenn, ob* the verb goes to the very end:

- *Ich weiß, **dass** du Deutsch **lernst**.* — I know that you learn German.
- *Ich lerne Deutsch, **weil** es interessant **ist**.* — I learn German because it is interesting.

## Practice

Start with simple sentences. Change word order, but keep the verb second:

1. **Heute** *trinke* ich Kaffee.
2. **Kaffee** *trinke* ich heute.
3. **Ich** *trinke* heute Kaffee.

Three variants, one meaning — the verb *trinke* is always second.
